Understanding Factors

1 Understanding the Factors

The two dimensions were constructed by factor analysis on a set of ten indicators. The 10 indicators utilized (five for each dimension) were chosen for technical reasons: in order to compare findings over time, we used indicators from all four waves of the Values Surveys. These 10 indications represent only a small subset of the numerous beliefs and values that these two dimensions elicit, and they are not necessarily the most sensitive indicators of these dimensions. They do an excellent job of accessing two really significant aspects of cross-cultural variance, but it is vital to remember that these specific items are simply indications of much wider underlying dimensions of cross-cultural variation.

1.1 Traditional Values (Secular values emphasizing the opposite [secular = positive pole]):

The traditional vs secular-rational values dimension highlights the difference between civilizations where religion is highly significant and those where it is not, yet obedience to God’s authority, fatherland, and family are all inextricably intertwined. In traditional societies, a major goal in most people’s lives is to make their parents proud; and one must always love and respect one’s parents regardless of how they behave; conversely, parents must do their best for their children, even if it means sacrificing their own well-being; and people idealize large families (and actually have them: high scores on this dimension correlate strongly with high fertility rates).

Traditional societies accept national authority passively: they rarely discuss politics, despite having high levels of national pride, favoring more respect for authority, taking protectionist attitudes toward foreign trade, and believing that environmental problems can be solved without international agreements. The family is critical to survival in preindustrial society. As a result, communities at the traditional pole of this dimension oppose divorce and advocate against abortion, euthanasia, and suicide. They value social conformity above personal striving, value obedience to authority, and have a strong sense of national pride and a patriotic viewpoint. On all of these problems, societies with secular-rational ideals have opposing choices.

Code 
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
import plotly.express as px

df = pd.read_csv('factor_secular.csv')

# PLOT
df = df.sort_values(by = 'secular')
fig = px.bar(df,y=df.iloc[:,0],x="secular", orientation='h')
fig.add_hrect(
        y0=2.5, y1=5, line_width=0, 
        fillcolor="green", opacity=0.1,
        annotation_text="Secular", annotation_position="top",
        )
fig.add_hrect(
        y0=2.5, y1=-1, line_width=0, 
        fillcolor="red", opacity=0.1,
        annotation_text="Traditional", annotation_position="bottom",
        )
fig.update_xaxes(visible=False)
fig.show()

1.2 Survival Values (Self-expression values emphasizing the opposite [self-expression = positive pole]):

The survival vs self-expression dimension taps into a postindustrial syndrome of tolerance, trust, emphasis on subjective well-being, civic involvement, and self-expression that occurs in societies with high levels of existential security and individual liberty. People in societies shaped by existential insecurity and rigid intellectual and social constraints on human autonomy, on the other hand, prioritize economic and physical security over all else; they are threatened by foreigners, ethnic diversity, and cultural change, which leads to intolerance of gays and other outgroups, insistence on traditional gender roles, and an authoritarian political outlook. The polarization of materialist and postmaterialist ideals is a key component of this level.

These values reflect an intergenerational movement away from economic and physical security and toward a greater emphasis on self-expression, subjective well-being, and life quality. This cultural change may be recognized across postindustrial civilization; it arises among birth cohorts who have grown up in settings where survival is taken for granted. These ideals are connected to the growing emphasis on environmental conservation, the women’s movement, and increased demands for involvement in economic and political decision-making. These principles have been more prevalent in practically all postindustrial civilizations during the last thirty years. Societies that prioritize survival principles have low levels of subjective well-being, poor health, and low levels of interpersonal trust, intolerance of outgroups, and support for gender equality. They place an emphasis on materialist values, have a high level of confidence in science and technology, are low on environmental action, and prefer authoritarian rule. Societies that place a high priority on self-expression tend to have opposing preferences on all of these areas. Self-expression principles, in general, represent an emancipatory and humanistic ethos that emphasizes individual autonomy and choice. When survival is uncertain, cultural variety appears to be a threat. When there isn’t enough food to go around, foreigners are viewed as dangerous outsiders who may grab one’s food.

In an attempt to enhance predictability in an unpredictable environment, people adhere to established gender roles and sexual norms, stressing absolute laws and old familiar standards. When survival becomes a given, ethnic and cultural variety become more tolerable - fact, beyond a certain point, diversity is not only accepted but positively prized since it is intriguing and engaging. People in postindustrial countries seek out international restaurants to try different cuisines; they spend high prices and travel considerable distances to experience exotic cultures. Changing gender roles and sexual norms no longer appear to be a concern. The transition toward gender equality, which allows women to pick from a considerably larger range of life paths than ever before, has been one of the most profound cultural upheavals since the start of recorded history.

Polarization over new gender roles is a crucial component of the survival vs self-expression dimension; one of its most contentious problems is whether men or women make better political leaders. A majority of the globe still believes that males make better political leaders than women; however, this view is being challenged by expanding majorities in postindustrial economies, as well as by the younger generation within these cultures. Equal rights for women, homosexuals and lesbians, immigrants, and other outgroups are often rejected in civilizations where existence is precarious, but are increasingly embraced in communities that value self-expression. Thus, each of modernization’s two key phases - industrialization and the creation of postindustrial society - gives birth to a significant component of cross-cultural diversity.
